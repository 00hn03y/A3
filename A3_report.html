Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The golden ratio spiral is visually striking and engaging, making it enjoyable to follow the timeline chronologically from the center outward.
  • Significant surges in new COVID-19 cases appear in: October 2020 – January 2021, August 2021 – November 2021, and January 2022 onward.
  • While these spikes are prominent, the overall absolute case numbers in late 2021 and 2022 remain significantly higher than in 2020, even at previous peaks.
  • The graph ends in January 2022 with an ominous, sharp rise, signaling an unprecedented surge in cases.

Design Critique:

  • The increasing radius of the spiral visually downplays the severity of peaks in late 2021 and 2022. A linear graph might better emphasize these surges.
  • The legend does not fully account for cases exceeding 150K, despite thicker lines suggesting higher numbers.
  • Additional context—such as death rates, cumulative case counts, or key events—could enhance interpretation and provide deeper insights into trend shifts.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

My final visualization highlights the severity of COVID-19 case surges in 2021, while preserving the cyclical nature of pandemic waves. By juxtaposing a circular graph and a linear graph, my design effectively captures both the seasonality of surges and the absolute increase in case numbers throughout 2021. The circular graph maps time onto an angle to emphasize recurring winter spikes, while the linear graph provides a direct numerical comparison, ensuring that 2021’s case severity is not visually understated. In the circular graph, a time period slider allows for dynamic interaction, helping users explore case trends over different periods. Line thickness increases during peak periods, reinforcing the impact of surges, and the uniform radius per year prevents the misrepresentation of 2021’s higher case counts. In the linear graph, I annotate key dates of variant surges for extra contexts. The graph also layers 7-day average cases and cumulative cases to highlight again the accelerated speed of COVID-19 surges in 2021.

While this approach effectively communicates seasonality, severity, and context, it abstracts fine daily fluctuations and requires some interpretation, particularly for users unfamiliar with circular graphs. Despite these trade-offs, my combination of visual encodings—position, color, annotations, and interactivity—narrates the accelerating spread of COVID-19 in 2021 while preserving the cyclical nature of case surges.

Reflection

My final design attempts to address many of my critiques, particularly in terms of preserving seasonality, accurately representing the severity of 2021 cases, and providing contextual explanations for case surges. By moving away from the golden spiral and instead using a circular graph with a fixed radius per year, I’ve mitigated the issue of visually downplaying the scale of 2021’s cases. I added a linear graph that represents both 7-day average and cumulative cases, to provide a clearer comparison between 2020 and 2021, ensuring that the dramatic rise in cases is more evident than in the original spiral design. Annotations and interactive tooltips enhance interpretability by connecting variant surges to fluctuations in case numbers.

However, the process of critique by redesign also revealed certain trade-offs and design tensions. While the circular graph effectively conveys seasonality, it inevitably abstracts some fine daily variations that a purely linear time-series graph would better display. Additionally, balancing the dual visualization approach—where both circular and linear graphs coexist—introduced challenges in ensuring the two remained clearly interpretable without overwhelming the user. The need to adjust scale and avoid data overlap further highlighted the difficulties of designing a visualization that is both aesthetically compelling and analytically precise. Ultimately, while the redesign significantly improves clarity and accuracy, it also underscores the inherent trade-offs in visualizing complex, time-dependent data—where emphasizing one aspect (e.g., seasonality) may come at the expense of another (e.g., granularity in daily trends).
